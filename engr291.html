Introduction

There may appear to be a lot of content here, but basically it boils down to this: to complete this course, you need to attend CV and interview workshops, prepare and submit a professional CV and attend three industry events on topics you find interesting. Oh, and you will also need to write a report on those three industry events.

You may be fortunate enough to get work over summer. This is not typical so don't stress if you don't - industry typically prefers third year students over second year - but if you do find work it can usually be counted towards your 800 hours. Typically you will undertake this work experience over summer when you have no coursework; and shortly after your work experience is completed you will need to submit a report. As your work experience is an academic exercise, your report helps us to establish what learning has been achieved along the way. The way you demonstrate your learning in this context is through reflecting on your experience in relation to your learning goals. The good news here is that you get to pick your learning goals yourself. Of course, should you choose not to set yourself any learning goals, we will expect that you intend to learn ALL THE THINGS - and your final report should reflect this.

Please note: attempting to learn ALL THE THINGS over one summer is not recommended.

So, two workshops, create a CV and three industry events - some even provide pizza! - followed by interesting work over summer and getting paid for that work. We should charge you for this course!

Due date for all of this is the last day of trimester 2, before the exam period.

Any questions, pop in to see Matt (CO354) or email Matt.Stevens@ecs.vuw.ac.nz

Lectures

There is only one set lecture for this course which aims to set the context for the work experience program.

Attend a CV writing workshop

To be successful in finding work which will count towards the Work Experience component of your BE degree, you will need to produce a CV that persuades employers that they want to interview you.

There are a number of options available to help you with preparing a CV.

Attending a CV writing workshop is a mandatory requirement. Typically the organiser will supply ECS with a list of attendees - if you are not on the list, you may have to redo the workshop.

Prepare a professional CV

Submit a CV within two weeks of attending a CV workshop and submit your CV here. You may be provided with feedback which will mean resubmitting.

Attend an Interview Skills workshop

Once you've got an interview for a job, you will want to feel confident about the interview process. You will need to attend a workshop where you can get some ideas of how it's done and a chance to try it out.

Attending an Interview workshop is a mandatory requirement. Again, the organiser will supply ECS with a list of attendees - and if you are not on the list, you may have to redo the workshop.

Attend a minimum of three industry events

Industry events offer two main benefits:

  • They put you in the same room as people with similar interests to you, that already work where you want to be working. These are people who want to chat about new technologies they are using or want to try. They like ECS students, and some may know about student job and internship opportunities.

  • They give you a range of interesting topics to talk about during interviews. They are typically technical in nature, not part of your studies and show a willingness to engage with industry communities.

What sort of events can I use for this?

These should be industry-based events that involve you engaging with people that are currently working or have worked in the IT industry.

Attending students should be outnumbered by non-students. This therefore excludes workshops or lectures where one or two industry people are engaging with many students, such as CV or interview workshops.

The three events should not duplicate. For example, only one industry 'Meet and Greet' event or only one 'Meetup' with a Ruby on Rails group (note, however, that there are also other alternatives including Python and Java groups, Continuous Integration, etc.). You can of course keep returning to groups you like, but only one attendance counts for your reflective report.

IT industry Meetups happen every week, year-round. There are over 100 tech groups in Wellington on topics as diverse as your favourite language or framework, to IoT, js, css, robotics, blockchain, AWS, UX, game developers, Docker, OWASP, Agile - and many more.

Feel free to ask your Work Experience Coordinator if your chosen event counts. The guiding principle is whether it puts you in direct contact with people that are working or have worked in the local IT industry. Attending ITP events, a hackathon weekend, a Creative HQ startup workshop, tech meetups, are all valid examples.

Optional Practical Work Experience

You don't need work experience to pass ENGR291, as the work experience component is typically recorded in ENGR391 and ENGR491.

However, if you have already secured or completed some related work you may complete the Work Experience Forms and Reports per the link below. Note that your work experience should be approved in advance of you starting. If you feel you may be eligible for an exception, see your Work Experience Coordinator.

  • Work Experience Instructions

Completing ENGR291

When you get to this point you should have completed the requirements for ENGR291. You should have:

  • attended CV writing and Interview Skills workshops
  • prepared a professional CV
  • attended three different industry events
  • written a short reflective report on the three industry events attended
